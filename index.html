II. Reading

A Day Without Internet

Last week, a major technical failure cut internet access across the city for almost twelve hours. We spoke to three people about how they spent that day.

Sofia Chen, 29

It was a disaster. I'm a freelance designer and I work from home, so I need the internet for everything. I had three deadlines that day, and suddenly I couldn't send any files or contact my clients. I tried using my phone as a hotspot, but the mobile network was also very slow. I kept thinking, "If this continues, I'll lose my clients." I sent a quick message to everyone explaining the situation, but not all of them were understanding. In the end, I went to a café that had a private connection, but I had to pay a lot to use it. I kept all my receipts. When I finally got home that night, I was completely exhausted.

David Park, 41

Situations like this can be stressful, but they don't have to be. The key is to stay calm. I've noticed that people often get angry when technology stops working because they feel out of control, which is uncomfortable. But getting angry doesn't solve anything. I was preparing a lecture when the internet went down. I decided to use the time differently. I read two chapters of a book I'd been trying to finish for months. I even went for a walk and had lunch in the park. When the internet came back, I felt refreshed and finished my work quickly.

Valentina Rojas, 22

I was at the university library when it happened. Everyone around me started complaining immediately. But honestly, I found it quite exciting. I had my textbooks with me, so I studied without any distractions for hours. Usually, I check my phone every few minutes, but that day I simply couldn't. I got more work done in four hours than I normally do in a whole day. I also talked to students I'd never spoken to before, and we even organized a study group. I think we should all disconnect from technology more often.
